# Dead Plate: The Browser Horror Game Hiding Inside a Fancy Restaurant

Some horror games announce themselves with a monster on the title screen. Dead Plate does the opposite. It opens with a job interview, a clean apron, and the kind of upscale bistro you would be lucky to work in. The lights are warm, the chef is charming, and the worst thing you expect from the evening is a customer who sends back the soup. That calm is the bait, and by the time the week is over it has hooked a surprising number of horror fans.


[Dead Plate](https://deadplate.app "Dead Plate") has quietly become a favorite among players who like their scares slow, character-driven, and a little bit funny. It trades cheap jump scares for dread you can almost taste, and it does so inside a setting most games would treat as background noise: a working kitchen in 1960s France.

## A normal job that goes very wrong

You step into the shoes of a young waiter on his first real shift at a restaurant called La Gueule de Saturne. The owner, a sharp-dressed chef, has built a reputation that packs the dining room every night, and your task is to keep that machine humming: greet guests, take orders, deliver plates, and pocket as much as the week allows.

For a while it plays exactly like the small-business fantasy it pretends to be. Then the routine starts to itch. A guest lingers a beat too long. A door stays shut for no clear reason. The kitchen, which should be the loudest room in any restaurant, goes strangely quiet. The clever part of the writing is how long it lets you ignore those signals — because you are busy, because you want the tips, because the boss is so easy to like. By the time the truth arrives, you are already part of the evening's rhythm.

## Two people you will not forget

The whole of Dead Plate rests on a pair of characters, and the game is smart enough to keep the cast small. The waiter is anxious, eager, and a little too willing to assume the best about everyone around him. The chef is the gravity in the room — funny, confident, and impossible to read until it is far too late.

![Dead Plate character key art of the waiter and the chef](https://s.cobb-can-move.com/games/dead-plate/dead-plate-poster.webp)
*The two leads carry the entire story between them.*

Their dynamic does most of the heavy lifting. The script runs on thousands of lines of dialogue, and the real tension sits in the gap between what one of them says and what he is plainly doing. Watching that gap widen is more unsettling than any creature could ever be. There are no filler party members and no padded side quests here — just two people, one restaurant, and a single week that goes somewhere terrible.

## What Dead Plate looks like

Before any of the horror lands, the art has already set the trap. Dead Plate leans into a moody, hand-drawn 1960s palette: deep reds, candle-warm light, and ornate frames around faces that always seem one bad night away from cracking. It feels less like a video game and more like a vintage menu you found in a drawer and probably should have left there.

Dozens of illustrated moments mark the turning points, so even once you sense where the story is heading, the presentation keeps catching you off guard. A short game with this much visual personality is rare, and it is a big part of why screenshots of this restaurant keep showing up across horror corners of the internet.

## Restaurant management, with a knife under the table

Mechanically, Dead Plate wears the clothes of a tiny tycoon sim. You move with the arrow keys, interact with a single button, and sprint when the dinner rush stacks up. Tables fill, orders pile, patience drains, and you are constantly deciding which small fire to put out first.

![Dead Plate controls and interaction screen](https://s.cobb-can-move.com/games/dead-plate/dead-plate-5.webp)

What keeps it from feeling like a throwaway phone game is the point-and-click curiosity layered on top. Parts of the scenery are clickable, and poking at the world instead of rushing the objective is how you uncover the details that actually matter. The serving loop gives the horror a heartbeat — every plate you run is one more tick of the clock — and that steady pressure makes the quiet, wrong moments hit so much harder.

## Four endings worth chasing

A single run of Dead Plate is short, somewhere around an hour to ninety minutes, and that brevity is a feature rather than a flaw. The game is built to be replayed. Across multiple sittings you can reach four distinct endings, each one recoloring scenes you thought you had figured out and tucking away small secrets for players who slow down and look.

That structure is a big reason Dead Plate keeps circulating in horror communities. People finish one ending, immediately realize what they missed, and jump back in to test a different choice. It is the rare short title where the second run is often better than the first, because you finally understand what you were really watching the whole time.

## Where to play Dead Plate without downloading anything

The easiest way to try it is straight from your browser. A fan-built page lets you launch the full game in a frame, press a button, and start your shift with no installer and no storefront detour. You can [play Dead Plate online at deadplate.app](https://deadplate.app), where the browser build sits alongside screenshots, gameplay videos, and a short FAQ for anyone who gets stuck.

A keyboard is strongly recommended, so a laptop or desktop is the comfortable way in. The page reads fine on a phone, but the arrow-key movement really wants physical keys. And if the frame takes a moment to wake up, give it a few seconds before refreshing — remote game builds occasionally need a beat to finish loading.

## Who should and should not play it

A bit of honest warning: Dead Plate is a mature horror story, and it does not soften that fact. The game itself opens with a content notice covering graphic death, blood, gore, violence, cannibalism, and disturbing imagery, and it earns every one of those words. If you are after a cozy cooking game to unwind with, this is not the one to load up before bed.

But if you enjoy horror that builds its characters first and then quietly removes the floor beneath them, it is hard to beat for a single evening. The craft is all in the patience — the way an ordinary restaurant shift slowly turns into a trap you helped set without noticing.

## The short version

Dead Plate is proof that you do not need a blockbuster budget or a haunted mansion to genuinely unsettle people. You need a believable job, two characters you cannot stop thinking about, and the nerve to let the dread simmer until it boils over. Pour yourself something, set aside an hour, and clock in for one shift.

Just remember that the most dangerous thing in this restaurant was never on the menu — and when you are ready to start, the browser version is waiting for you at [deadplate.app](https://deadplate.app).
